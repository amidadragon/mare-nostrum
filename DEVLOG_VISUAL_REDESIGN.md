### Written Devlog (itch.io / blog format)

---

**Title:** The visual redesign — what changed and why

---

I've been quiet about Mare Nostrum for the past few weeks because I was knee-deep in something I didn't want to talk about until I had something to show.

The game needed a visual overhaul. Not because the old art was broken — it worked — but because it didn't feel like anything. It was functional placeholder with some nice lighting effects bolted on. For a game whose entire emotional pitch is "cozy, warm, you belong here," that's a problem you can't solve with features.

So I went back to the beginning.

**The central question**

The art direction now starts with a single question: what does a *solarpunk* Mediterranean actually look like?

Mare Nostrum isn't the Rome of conquest, marble, and empire. The story is about a Roman exile who builds something small and good in a place they weren't supposed to survive. The world needed to reflect that. Warm, alive, in balance with its islands. Not triumphant. Habitable.

That shifted everything.

**Every tile, from scratch**

The beach sand picks up morning light differently from afternoon light. The stone buildings read differently from the wooden ones — different warmth, different reflectivity in the pixel sense. The underwater bioluminescence is a specific teal-green that took a while to land on — bright enough to feel alive, dark enough to feel deep.

The sky at hour 19 — the golden hour before night — is the one I'm most pleased with. It's a specific, deliberate amber that we just kept working on until it felt right. When NPCs stand in that light and you can see the pollen particles floating through it, the scene finally looks like the game I was trying to make.

**The character skins**

This is the part I'm most excited about.

When you wash ashore in Chapter I, you look the part. Torn cloth, disheveled, clearly not doing well. That's the castaway skin. It's intentional — you're at the bottom, and you look it.

As your colony grows and your island levels up through the 25-level progression, your character visually changes. Citizen tunic. Governor's robes with a red border. Senator's toga. Finally, the Imperator's laurel.

It's not a cosmetic unlock system. There's no menu for it. It just happens — you cross a level threshold and the next time you load or transition, you look different. The idea is that it functions as a mirror. You can glance at yourself and know roughly where you are in the story. And in the final payoff shot — standing in Imperator's laurel with the Imperial Bridge visible behind you — the transformation from that first shipwrecked sprite on the beach is the entire emotional arc of the game, visible without a word.

**What's still the same**

The procedural audio didn't change. The lyre synthesizing live in Dorian mode while you farm, shifting to Phrygian when enemies appear, dropping to sparse reverbed tones in the Necropolis — that's all still there. The 10-chapter narrative is intact. The NPCs, the companion AI critters, the Imperial Bridge, the volcanic forge — all still in.

The redesign is purely visual, but visual is where you spend most of your time.
