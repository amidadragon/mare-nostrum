# Mare Nostrum — Competitive Analysis Report

**Date:** 2026-03-21
**Target:** $4.99 on itch.io | Browser (p5.js) | ~45K lines

---

## 1. Competitor Breakdown

### Stardew Valley

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Price** | $14.99 (no paid DLC — all updates free) |
| **Sales** | 50M+ copies, $518M gross revenue |
| **Steam Rating** | Overwhelmingly Positive (97%) |
| **Platform** | PC, Console, Mobile |

**Core loop (every 5 min):** Wake up → tend crops → choose activity (mine/fish/socialize/forage) → sell/upgrade → sleep. Each day ~15 min real-time. The daily structure forces micro-decisions about time allocation.

**Retention hook:** Layered progression — crops grow over days, relationships deepen over weeks, seasons rotate, Community Center bundles create long-arc goals. The "one more day" effect comes from crops about to harvest, a birthday tomorrow, or a festival approaching.

**First 30 minutes:** Cinematic intro (corporate escape fantasy), immediate farm ownership, free seeds to plant, fishing rod gifted via cutscene, town to explore with 30+ NPCs. Multiple hooks deployed simultaneously — farming, fishing, foraging, social.

**What players love:** Freedom, relaxing pace, depth hidden beneath simplicity, no microtransactions, solo-dev authenticity.

**What players hate:** Energy system early-game feels restrictive. Mining combat is weak. Late-game can feel "solved."

**Key takeaway:** Stardew proves a single developer can build a billion-dollar franchise with tight loops, generous content, and zero predatory monetization.

---

### Cult of the Lamb

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Price** | $24.99 (cosmetic DLC $4.99-$6.99, Woolhaven expansion $16.99) |
| **Sales** | 4.5M+ copies, ~$90M lifetime revenue |
| **Steam Rating** | Very Positive (85%) |
| **Platform** | PC, Console |

**Core loop (every 5 min):** 10-min dungeon crusade → return to base → manage followers (feed, heal, inspire, sacrifice) → build/upgrade cult → crusade again. The two halves create tension: you can't stay in either mode too long.

**Retention hook:** Roguelike variety (random weapons/curses per run) + base sim urgency (followers starve/dissent while you're gone). Unlockable doctrines and rituals give permanent progression.

**First 30 minutes:** Boss fight immediately, rescue followers, establish cult base, first crusade. Action-first onboarding — no slow burn.

**What players love:** Art style, dark humor, satisfying combat, the push-pull between dungeon and base.

**What players hate:** Combat lacks depth vs. Hades. Base management can overshadow dungeons. Late-game repetition.

**Key takeaway:** Combining two genres (roguelike + sim) works when each half feeds the other. But both halves need enough depth to sustain 20+ hours.

---

### Moonlighter

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Price** | $19.99 (Between Dimensions DLC ~$6.99) |
| **Sales** | 2M-5M owners on Steam |
| **Steam Rating** | Very Positive (80%) |
| **Platform** | PC, Console, Mobile |

**Core loop (every 5 min):** Dungeon crawl → collect loot → return to shop → price items (watch customer reactions) → sell → upgrade gear/shop → deeper dungeon. The pricing mechanic is the unique hook — trial-and-error to find optimal prices.

**Retention hook:** Each dungeon run funds the next upgrade. Shop upgrades are visible and satisfying. Town grows as you invest.

**First 30 minutes:** Dungeon tutorial, collect items, open shop next morning, experiment with pricing. The shop mechanic feels novel immediately.

**What players love:** Shop management novelty, pixel art, the dual-identity fantasy (shopkeeper by day, adventurer by night).

**What players hate:** Combat becomes repetitive. Dungeon generation feels samey. Economy gets trivial once you learn prices.

**Key takeaway:** A single novel mechanic (dynamic pricing) can carry a game — but the supporting systems need enough depth to not feel like filler.

### Spiritfarer

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Price** | $29.99 (Farewell Edition — free content updates, no paid DLC) |
| **Sales** | 1M+ copies |
| **Steam Rating** | Overwhelmingly Positive (95%) |
| **Platform** | PC, Console, Mobile |

**Core loop (every 5 min):** Sail to island → gather resources → build/upgrade ship rooms → cook food for spirits → talk to spirits → advance their storylines → eventually say goodbye at the Everdoor.

**Retention hook:** Emotional investment in spirit characters. Each farewell is earned through hours of care. Players don't want to let go — which is the point.

**First 30 minutes:** Meet Charon, inherit the boat, rescue first spirit, build their house, cook their favorite meal, learn to hug them. Emotional hook set within 20 minutes.

**What players love:** Art (hand-drawn animation), music (orchestral), emotional storytelling, the hug mechanic, co-op.

**What players hate:** Backtracking becomes tedious. Resource gathering gets repetitive. Some spirits feel underdeveloped. Late-game pacing drags.

**Key takeaway:** Emotional resonance is the most powerful retention tool. Players will tolerate repetitive mechanics if they care about the characters. But pacing must stay tight.

---

### Littlewood

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Price** | $14.99 (no DLC) |
| **Sales** | 200K-500K owners on Steam |
| **Steam Rating** | Very Positive (93%) |
| **Platform** | PC, Switch |

**Core loop (every 5 min):** Gather resources (wood/stone/fish/bugs) → build/place buildings → talk to villagers → level up skills → unlock new areas. Every action costs time (not stamina), and the day ends after ~60 actions.

**Retention hook:** Post-adventure premise removes all pressure. Town customization is freeform. Museum collection and relationship systems provide completionist goals.

**First 30 minutes:** You're the amnesiac hero — the adventure already happened. Immediate town building, resource gathering, meeting characters. Zero combat, zero threat.

**What players love:** Zero pressure, creative town layout, charming pixel art, strong first 10 hours.

**What players hate:** Late-game grind, shallow NPC dialogue, quality drop in mid-to-late content. Characters lack personality depth.

**Key takeaway:** The "post-adventure" framing is brilliant — it removes the need for combat while maintaining stakes (rebuilding). But content depth must sustain through endgame.

---

### Forager

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Price** | $19.99 (no DLC) |
| **Sales** | 1M-2M owners on Steam, ~$33M gross revenue |
| **Steam Rating** | Very Positive (88%) |
| **Platform** | PC, Console, Switch |

**Core loop (every 5 min):** Harvest resources (auto-respawn) → craft items → build structures → unlock skill tree nodes → buy new land tiles → explore puzzles. Resources respawn constantly — there's always something to click.

**Retention hook:** Pure dopamine. Constant resource pops, XP dings, level-ups, unlocks. The screen is always full of things to collect. Idle-game DNA means progress even when you're "just standing there."

**First 30 minutes:** Immediate resource gathering on a tiny island. Craft first pickaxe within 2 minutes. Buy first land expansion within 10. Skill tree opens with clear upgrade paths. The dopamine hits start immediately and never stop.

**What players love:** Addictive loop, cute art, accessibility, "just one more unlock" pull.

**What players hate:** Late-game grind wall, island costs escalate too fast, shallow once the unlock tree is complete. Developer controversy (underpaying team, claiming solo dev status).

**Key takeaway:** Idle-game mechanics + exploration creates extreme engagement. But the endgame must have a payoff, not just exponentially increasing grind.

---

### Unpacking

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Price** | $19.99 (no DLC) |
| **Sales** | 100K+ in first 10 days, ~$30M gross revenue |
| **Steam Rating** | Overwhelmingly Positive (93%) |
| **Platform** | PC, Console, Mobile |

**Core loop (every 5 min):** Open box → examine item → find the right spot → place it → repeat. Each level is a new home at a new life stage. Story emerges entirely from what you unpack and where.

**Retention hook:** Environmental storytelling — players piece together a life story from objects. Curiosity drives completion. Each move reveals character growth (or regression).

**First 30 minutes:** Childhood bedroom. Unpack toys, books, stuffed animals. No tutorial needed — the mechanic is intuitive. By the second level (college dorm), players are already invested in the character's life.

**What players love:** Pixel art detail, ASMR-quality sound design, emotional storytelling without words, nostalgia, accessibility.

**What players hate:** Too short (3-5 hours). Price-to-length ratio ($20 for 3 hours). Strict item placement rules feel arbitrary.

**Key takeaway:** A $20 game with no combat, no progression system, and 3 hours of content can gross $30M if the emotional experience is strong enough. Presentation and polish matter more than feature count.

## 4. Specific Feature Recommendations

### Priority 1 — Ship This Week (Critical for Launch)

1. **Tighten the first 30 minutes.** Every successful competitor hooks players in under 20 minutes. The shipwreck opening is strong — make sure the player plants a crop, catches a fish, meets an NPC, and builds their first structure within the first session. Test this ruthlessly.

2. **Add "tomorrow hooks."** At the end of each day/session, show the player what's coming: "Your wheat is 1 day from harvest," "Hanno arrives tomorrow with rare goods," "Chapter 3 unlocks at dawn." Stardew and Forager both exploit this.

3. **Juice the feedback.** Floating damage numbers exist (combat), but every positive action should feel rewarding: harvest combos with escalating SFX, fishing catch celebrations, building completion fanfare. Forager proves that "screen feel" drives retention more than content volume.

### Priority 2 — First Update (Week 1-2 Post-Launch)

4. **Deepen NPC relationships.** Add gift preferences, daily schedules, and at least 3 unique dialogue events per NPC tied to narrative progress. The favor system is the skeleton — add the meat. Stardew's relationship system is its most-cited feature.

5. **Seasonal visual shifts.** Change island palette, crop availability, ambient sounds, and music mode per season. This is the cheapest way to make the world feel alive. Stardew's seasons are half its identity.

6. **Physical museum/collection display.** Let players place caught fish, harvested crops, and found artifacts in a visible space. Littlewood and Stardew both prove this drives 20+ extra hours of completionist play.

### Priority 3 — v1.1+ (Month 1-2 Post-Launch)

7. **Expand NPC roster to 8-10.** Four NPCs cannot sustain a colony fantasy. Add 2-3 recruitable characters per faction (Roman/Carthaginian) with unique skills and storylines.

8. **Post-narrative endgame.** After Chapter 10, add prestige goals: rival island conquest, legendary fish, arena leaderboards, colony beautification scoring. Every competitor fails at endgame — this is an opportunity to differentiate.

9. **Festival events.** 4 seasonal festivals with unique minigames (Roman Saturnalia, harvest feast, naval parade, etc.). Stardew's festivals are some of its most memorable moments.

| Moonlighter | $19.99 | 15-25 | $0.80-1.33 |
| Spiritfarer | $29.99 | 25-35 | $0.86-1.20 |
| Littlewood | $14.99 | 30-50 | $0.30-0.50 |
| Forager | $19.99 | 15-30 | $0.67-1.33 |
| Unpacking | $19.99 | 3-5 | $4.00-6.67 |
| **Mare Nostrum** | **$4.99** | **15-25 (est.)** | **$0.20-0.33** |

At $4.99, Mare Nostrum is priced at extreme value. This is correct for a browser-first itch.io launch — it removes all purchase friction and makes impulse buys trivial.

**Recommended monetization path:**
1. **Launch at $4.99** on itch.io with "pay what you want" minimum $4.99
2. **Track average payment** — if players regularly pay $7-10, the game is underpriced
3. **If Steam launch happens**, price at $9.99-$12.99 (Steam audience expects higher prices and discounts)
4. **Paid expansion** ($3.99-$4.99) if the game finds an audience: new island chain, new faction, expanded endgame
5. **Never** add microtransactions — the cozy game audience punishes this severely

## 6. Competitive Positioning Summary

**Mare Nostrum's unique angle:** It's the only cozy life-sim set in the ancient Mediterranean with a solarpunk twist, browser-playable, at a sub-$5 price point. No competitor occupies this niche.

**The real competition is not these games — it's attention.** At $4.99 browser-native, Mare Nostrum competes less with $20 Steam games and more with free web games, mobile games, and the 10 minutes someone has between tasks. The browser-native advantage is huge: zero friction from discovery to play.

**Win condition:** A player finds Mare Nostrum on itch.io, clicks play, and within 15 minutes is invested in their island, their crops, and the story. They come back tomorrow because their wheat is about to harvest and Chapter 2 just unlocked. They tell a friend because the procedural lyre music is unlike anything they've heard in a browser game. They pay $4.99 because they already played for an hour before the purchase prompt.

**Lose condition:** The first 30 minutes feel like a tech demo instead of a world. NPCs feel like quest dispensers instead of characters. The day ends and there's no reason to start the next one.

The gap to close is not features — Mare Nostrum already has more systems than most competitors. The gap is **emotional hooks** and **session-to-session compulsion**. Fix those two things and the game competes.
