### Long Description

---

**Shipwrecked. Sunlit. Reborn.**

You wake on a wreck-strewn beach. Salt in your lungs, planks scattered around you, the sun already burning gold off the water. You have nothing. Somewhere beyond the horizon is an island — and people — and a life worth rebuilding.

That's the beginning of Mare Nostrum. What happens next is up to you.

---

**What is this?**

Mare Nostrum is a cozy Roman life-sim / survival-crafting game set on floating Mediterranean islands. You are a Roman exile. You start with wreckage. You end as Imperator.

It plays in your browser, right now, with no install required.

If Stardew Valley sailed the Mediterranean and hired a Roman historian as narrative director, this is the game they'd make.

---

**The arc:**

Chapter I: Scavenge the wreck. Repair your trireme. Sail to safety.
Chapter III: Uncover Livia's secret — she's a senator's daughter, exiled for blood.
Chapter IV: Help Marcus find his lost legion's eagle standard buried on a wild island.
Chapter VI: Build the Imperial Bridge — stone by stone — across open water to your colony.
Chapter VII: Forge the Blade of Vulcan in a living volcano's crater.
Chapter IX: Enter the Necropolis. Read the final inscription. Learn why you were really exiled.
Chapter X: Perform the Rite of Mare Nostrum. Not by conquest — by love.

---

**Features**

- 10-chapter narrative quest chain from shipwrecked exile to Imperator
- 4 fully voiced NPCs with personal quest chains: Livia, Marcus, Vesta, Felix
- Island progression system: Citizen > Governor > Senator > Consul > Imperator (25 levels)
- Build and colonize a second island — then connect them with a walkable Imperial Bridge
- 4 explorable islands: Isle of Vulcan (volcanic forge), Hyperborea (frozen ruins), Isle of Plenty (spice trade), the Necropolis (answers)
- Farming, fishing, cooking, diving, combat, trade routes — interlocking systems that reward patience
- 4 autonomous AI companions with personality (cats, a tortoise, a crow)
- Procedural lyre music — every note is synthesized live, no audio files. The music shifts from Dorian to Phrygian as danger rises, and drops to sparse low tones in the underworld
- Full day/night cycle with seasons, weather, and solar energy mechanics
- Pixel art with a warm Mediterranean palette: soft golds, sunset oranges, bioluminescent teals at night
- Plays in desktop browser and mobile. Saves to localStorage — your exile persists

---

**Controls**

- WASD or arrow keys to move
- Click to move (desktop) or tap (mobile)
- E to interact with buildings, NPCs, and objects
- Left-click to attack
- Esc or menu button for inventory/journal

---

**About**

Mare Nostrum is a solo-developed browser game built on p5.js — no engine, no install, no storefront friction. The entire audio system is procedural: the lyre you hear while farming, the ambient waves and crickets, the combat percussion — all synthesized in real time.

The narrative leans into real Roman history: the Legion IX Hispana, Sol Invictus, exile as political weapon, the concept of "mare nostrum" (our sea) as both empire and belonging.

This is a game about rebuilding. About quiet mornings planting grain when everything else was taken from you. About what it means to build something that lasts.

**Post 3 — r/gamedev (technical angle)**

**Title:** My browser game uses fully procedural audio — no audio files at all. Here's how it works, and why I built it that way.

> Mare Nostrum is a cozy Roman life-sim I built solo on p5.js. The entire audio system is procedural via p5.sound — there are zero static audio files in the project.
>
> The lyre generates notes in real time using oscillators tuned to Dorian mode during peaceful gameplay, shifts to Phrygian during combat/conquest, and drops to sparse low Dorian in the Necropolis (the underworld island). Mode switching is automatic — the SoundManager polls game state every frame and crossfades.
>
> Ambient layers (waves, wind, birds, crickets) are also synthesized. Each island has its own ambient profile: Vulcan gets low-frequency rumble and random percussive pops, Hyperborea gets high sine-wave "wind," the Necropolis gets sparse reverbed tones.
>
> Why? No licensing headaches. No audio files to version control. The music responds to game state at a granular level (individual note timing, not just volume fades). And the final bundle is smaller.
>
> The game itself is a 10-chapter narrative with farming, colony building, combat, and diving — all in browser with no install. 23k lines of JS, no build step, localStorage saves.
>
> Happy to answer questions about the procedural audio implementation or the p5.js architecture.
